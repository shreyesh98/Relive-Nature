Tornado

A tornado forms from a large thunderstorm. Inside thunderclouds, warm, humid air rises, while cool air falls--along with rain or hail. These conditions can cause spinning air currents inside the cloud.

Wildfire

Wildfires are often caused by human activity or a natural phenomenon such as lightning, and they can happen at any time or anywhere. A wildfire is an unplanned fire that burns in a natural area such as a forest, grassland

Cyclone

Cyclones are wind storms accompanied with heavy rainfall at low-pressure areas. They are caused due to a continuous process of rising of hot air over the ocean surface. This vacant space is then occupied by the cool air around, which further heats up and rises.

Threats

Higher Temperature

Earth’s temperatures in 2015 were the hottest ever recorded. Why does this matter?
Because a change of even 1o Fahrenheit which may sound small can upset the delicate balance of ecosystems, and affect plants and animals that inhabit them.

Changing Landscapes & Wildlife Habitat

Rising temperatures and changing patterns of rainfall are changing where plants grow, and in the case of our oceans, encouraging the production of species that impact native ocean habitat. As landscapes and environments literally shift, wildlife must quickly adjust. Experts predict that ¼ of Earth’s species will be headed for destruction by 2050 if the warming trend continues at its current rate.

Rising Seas

As ocean waters warm, they expand, causing sea levels to rise. Melting glaciers compound the problem by dumping even more fresh water into the oceans. Rising seas threaten to flood low lying areas and islands, threaten dense coastal populations, eat away shorelines, damage property and destroy ecosystems such as flowering trees and wetlands that protect coasts against storms.

Increased Risk of Storms, Droughts & Floods

Climate change is intensifying drought, storms, and floods around the world. Where nature has been destroyed by development, communities are at risk from these intensified climate patterns. Scientists around the world are studying how nature can be a buffer for these intensified weather patterns
